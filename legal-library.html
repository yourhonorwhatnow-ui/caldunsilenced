⚖️ Victorian Law

Intervention Orders in Victoria — A Plain Language Guide

What they are, how to get one, what happens at court, and what your rights are throughout the process.

What is an Intervention Order?

An Intervention Order (IVO) is a court order that legally protects you from someone who has harmed you or made you fear for your safety. In Victoria, they are governed by the Family Violence Protection Act 2008 (Vic). An IVO can stop the other person from:

  • Coming to your home, workplace, or children's school
  • Contacting you by phone, text, email, or social media
  • Coming within a certain distance of you
  • Committing any further family violence against you
💡 You do not need to be married or in a relationship with someone to get an IVO against them. It also covers family members, housemates, and carers.

Who can get one?

You can apply for an IVO if you are experiencing family violence from a current or former partner, a family member, someone you have a family-like relationship with, or a carer. Family violence includes physical violence, sexual assault, emotional abuse, financial abuse, controlling behaviour, threats, stalking, and property damage.

How do I apply?

  1. Go to your local Magistrates' Court and ask to apply for an Intervention Order. Court staff can help you fill in the forms — no lawyer needed.
  2. A magistrate will consider your application, often the same day. If there is immediate risk, they can make an Interim Order right away.
  3. The other person is served (notified) of the order and a final hearing date is set.
  4. At the final hearing, the magistrate decides whether to make the order permanent.
⚠️ There is no fee to apply for an Intervention Order. If anyone tells you there is a cost, contact Victoria Legal Aid immediately on 1300 792 887.

What if I don't speak English well?

You have the right to an interpreter in court — free of charge. When you contact the court, ask for an interpreter in your language. Victoria Legal Aid also has interpreters available — call 1300 792 887.

Emergency situations

If you are in immediate danger, call 000. Police can apply for an IVO on your behalf — you do not have to go to court yourself. Police are required under Victorian law to consider applying for an IVO whenever they attend a family violence incident.

🛡️ Your Rights

Victims Charter Act 2006 (Vic) — What It Means for You

The rights the law gives you as a victim of crime in Victoria — plain and simple.

What is the Victims Charter?

The Victims Charter Act 2006 (Vic) sets out the rights and entitlements of victims of crime in Victoria. It tells police, prosecutors, and courts how they must treat you. The Charter creates legal obligations — not just guidelines.

Your key rights under the Charter

  • To be treated with dignity, respect, and compassion
  • To have your privacy protected as much as possible
  • To be given information about the investigation and any charges
  • To be told about support services available to you
  • To have your views considered at key decisions in the case
  • To be told about bail conditions and court dates
  • To be notified when an offender is released from prison
  • To make a Victim Impact Statement if the offender is found guilty

What is a Victim Impact Statement?

A Victim Impact Statement (VIS) is a written or spoken account of how the crime affected you. You can write it in your own language, and it can be translated and read in court. You do not have to attend court — you can ask for it to be read by someone else.

💡 If you feel your rights under the Victims Charter have not been respected, contact the Victims of Crime Commissioner on (03) 8608 5971.
💰 Financial Support

VOCAT — Getting Financial Help as a Victim of Crime

The Victims of Crime Assistance Tribunal explained — who qualifies, how to apply, and what you can receive.

What is VOCAT?

VOCAT — the Victims of Crime Assistance Tribunal — can provide financial assistance to victims of violent crime under the Victims of Crime Assistance Act 1996 (Vic). It is government assistance, not compensation from the offender.

💡 You can apply for VOCAT even if you did not report the crime to police, and even if the offender was not charged or convicted.

What can VOCAT pay for?

  • Counselling and psychological treatment
  • Medical expenses related to the crime
  • Safety-related expenses (locks, security)
  • Lost earnings if you could not work due to the crime
  • Pain and suffering in some circumstances

How do I apply?

  1. Download the VOCAT application form from the Magistrates' Court of Victoria website, or collect one at any Magistrates' Court.
  2. Complete the form with details of the crime, your injuries, and the expenses you are claiming. A legal aid lawyer can help for free.
  3. Lodge the form at any Magistrates' Court — there is no fee.
  4. A VOCAT registrar assesses your application and may request more information or medical reports.
⚠️ Time limit: You must apply within 2 years of the crime occurring. In some circumstances (especially childhood abuse) this limit can be extended — contact a lawyer for advice.
🌍 Migration & Visas

Migration & Visa Protections — Your Status Is Not Controlled by Your Partner

If you are on a partner visa or temporary visa and experiencing family violence, you have rights.

The Family Violence Provisions

Australian migration law contains Family Violence Provisions that protect visa holders experiencing family violence. If your relationship breaks down due to family violence, you may still be able to stay in Australia and obtain permanent residency.

💡 Your partner cannot unilaterally cancel your visa. Only the Department of Home Affairs can cancel a visa. Threats about your visa are a form of abuse.

Key protections

  • If you are on a Partner visa and experiencing violence, you can apply separately for permanent residency under the family violence provisions
  • Evidence does not need to come from a court — statutory declarations, medical records, letters from support workers all count
  • You can access Medicare, crisis accommodation, and Centrelink payments even on a temporary visa in many circumstances

Get specialist help

  • inTouch Multicultural Centre — 1800 755 988 (specialist CALD DV support)
  • Victoria Legal Aid — 1300 792 887 (immigration legal advice)
  • Refugee Legal — (03) 9413 0101
⚠️ Migration law is complex. Please seek legal advice specific to your visa type before making major decisions.
📋 Legislation

Family Violence Protection Act 2008 (Vic) — The Basics

Understanding Victoria's main family violence law and what it means for your situation.

What does this law do?

The Family Violence Protection Act 2008 (Vic) is Victoria's primary family violence law. It defines what family violence is, establishes the Intervention Order system, and sets out how police and courts must respond.

How does the law define family violence?

  • Physical abuse — hitting, pushing, choking, burning, using weapons
  • Sexual abuse — any unwanted sexual acts or coercion
  • Emotional or psychological abuse — threats, intimidation, humiliation, isolation, controlling behaviour
  • Economic abuse — controlling money, stopping you from working, running up debts in your name
  • Stalking — following, monitoring, or tracking you
  • Exposing children to family violence — witnessing violence is itself family violence against the child
💡 Cultural practices do not justify family violence. The law applies equally to everyone in Victoria regardless of cultural background, religion, or nationality.
🏛️ Court Rights

Your Rights in Court — Evidence Act 2008 (Vic)

What you need to know if you are a witness or victim giving evidence in a Victorian court.

Special measures available to you

  • CCTV — giving evidence from another room via video link so you don't have to see the accused
  • Screen — a screen placed so you cannot see the accused while giving evidence
  • Support person — having someone sit with you while you give evidence
  • Suppression orders — keeping your name out of the media
  • Pre-recorded evidence — in some cases your evidence can be recorded before the trial

Protection from certain questions

In sexual assault cases, the defence cannot ask you questions about your prior sexual history without special permission from the judge. This protects you from re-traumatisation and victim blaming in court.

Interpreters in court

You have the right to give evidence through an interpreter in your preferred language — free of charge. Tell the prosecutor or court registrar as early as possible that you need an interpreter.

💡 You can ask for special measures before your court date — you do not have to wait until you are in the courtroom.
